27. Calibration

Adrift again. Falling, more like sinking. That’s how water worked, and this was sort of like that. Tossed straight into the abyss, not what she wanted. Her stone sank in an impossible pond, surprisingly shallow and unnervingly deep. Everything smelled like colors. She could taste them too. Rainbows were not delicious, red stung like blood and dental pain. The candy industry lied; she wanted a refund for her whole life. She’d call to complain, but she had no mouth, or anything else, and no one was here to listen.

Alone again. This wasn’t how it was supposed to work. Something about having a guide. Whatever this was, it was a dumb idea, so of course it came from her boss.

What was it again? The idea? The one that wasn’t working. Nora tried to replay how she’d arrived. Followed a meeting link that took her across the bay, over the gateway bridge. No, the grey one. Entered a tower, edges like sharp glass, familiar from a distance, alien novelty inside. Zaree got them in. Made a telephone call, spoke the magic words: free consultation. Opened the door. Like a hacker, or a wizard. Nora should’ve been impressed, but now she was just pissed.

She remembered seeing herself when she stepped through the door. It was only the first weird feeling. Except it wasn’t her, rather a shadow of her old otherself, flesh stitched from fresh matterprinter resin. Its sharp smell stung her nose, brought back memories. She’d had one of these on her desk, tiny, in a shoebox somewhere now. This was lifesize, like life, only in need of a fresh coat.

Mayalinn.

It was a puppet. No, Nora was the puppet, this was a statue. Maya triumphant. Saved the city, never mind the backstory. Center of the verse, eye of the storm. That’s where she was right now, where her shadow had stayed.

Jack. Must’ve been his idea. Hijacked Zaree’s good intentions, met them at the door. Hi, Jack. Good one, save that for later. Printed a Mayalinn to impress her, stuck it in the entrance of his tower to greet her. See a statue of yourself in the lobby, how can you say no? Trojan horse in reverse; gift got her past his gate, now trapped inside.

Meant he needed her for something. She fell for it, so stupid in retrospect. This whole trip had been his idea.

Trip. Inverse, in-verse, inside-out and inverted in the verse.

This was drugs. Except Nora didn’t do drugs, not so hot about video games anymore. Now she was on drugs, inside Jack’s video game. At least she could say it was for work.

Abhishek called it a great opportunity. They’d do some consulting work for Sandstorm’s new MI, pro bono. Nora could get certified for exascale. KorBridge would get in the door, the partnership her boss always wanted. The mythical win-win-win, as long as you weren’t the pro being bonoed.

No bueno. Felt like she was losing the game. Remembered sinking through the floor, into the dungeon. No, the Basement. Took the elevator down after the business talk. Blah blah, Zaree’s PowerPoint: KorBridge was here to help. Turns out Nora was here to get used. In the basement, she went into the white room. So clean, so smooth. Like her brain right now, no bumps at all. This was supposed to be a short trip, intro dose. Felt more like a soft reboot. Maybe if she wasn’t the only person in this fucking town who hadn’t tripped before. Not calibrated properly. Calibrated. Caleb. Guy who gave her the drugs. Kind looks. Calm, like a cult leader, handed her the cup with her tablet. Like the guy giving out the kool-aid.

That’s what she was swimming in, tasting it in her mouth. Not blood anymore, bitter iron swapped for fake sweet, pure red. But it meant she had a mouth now. Progress.

Stopped sinking, started swimming. Can’t swim without arms, meant she had those too. Very cool. Moving up now, not drowning in her own impulse. Might start thinking in complete sentences someday, be a whole person again. That’d be great, being something other than a shadow.

Just don’t get invested, sold into Jack’s scheme. Don’t get hooked like a phish.

Saw her shadow as she rose up out of the pool, crawled into the corner of the cell. Needed legs to crawl; cool, crossed those off the list of missing limbs. The corner was cold, stone walls, she’d been put in her place. Somewhere familiar yet forgotten.

Turned back to the puddle she crawled out of, no rainbow, just a leaky crypt. Turned again, saw Caleb standing over her. Priest robes, stone grey, red stole. Skull necklace, strange beads in his beard. Doom Cleric. Servant of the Sleeping Gods. Made sense, or was her mind making it all up?

She had legs but was still trying to find her feet. Worn boots, wasn’t naked. That’d be weird. “This is too much. This was supposed to be a short trip,” she said.

“Jack said you were a hero. You said you were in a hurry. I gave you the heroic dose,” Caleb admitted without malice.

“Could’ve killed me,” she replied, tasting spite on her tongue, another flavor of red. She’d forgotten about the urgency. Was this her idea? Better to keep blaming Jack or Abhishek; ideas this bad were always management’s fault.

“There is no known lethal limit. Believe me, I’d be dead,” Caleb said in shallow tones.

“Still, I’m not familiar with pharmaceuticals. Barely take Tylenol.” Then she remembered something about someone being sick.

“But you are quite familiar with the Stormverse, and that’s half the battle. Where are we, Nora?”

She looked around the room. A rusty rapier rested against unfinished brick. Looked down, torn vest, tattered leggings, scuffed boots. Fingerless gloves falling apart. “The Queen’s Stockades,” she said. Shadowblades started as thieves before learning magic later. It’d been so long she’d forgotten her own origin story. Mayalinn’s story. She was her, the shadow she left behind, same as the statue Jack used for his scheme. “I’m in prison for a crime I didn’t commit.”

The symbolism wasn’t lost, neither was she, not anymore. Starter dungeon, simple layout, shaped like a T. As if floating from above, she could see it in her mind. That’s what this was, her mind’s eye negotiating a connection. Building a link, laying down a protocol. Her sensorium syncing up with StormGEN-03. The new new user experience.

Her fingers reached for the rapier’s grip. With weapon in hand, ghostly hieroglyphics flickered just out of view—inch-high icons of slashing swords, magic missiles, little lightning bolts all off to the left. Training wheels, or just a joke? Her attention brought them center stage. Action buttons, the old interface, before haptics got good, before grip controllers translated every interaction. Every wish. Absolutely archaic in the age of AI. “I don’t need the tutorial,” she said to Caleb, banishing the buttons back to the periphery.

“You do. You might have wasted years of your life in the verse, but you never took a fencing lesson. And you’re not a wizard, no matter how often you still imagine yourself to be.”

How could he know? Was this guy for real or another case of Nora’s mind mocking itself? Either way, he was inside her head.

Time to get him out. Trying to strike, she swished at air. Clumsy dork, felt like a fool.

“Trust me Nora, this isn’t a puzzle you can figure out in five minutes. You need to build muscle memory, learn your limits, associate intention with action. You’re not the first person I’ve taken on the tour.”

“Did you throw them all in your dungeon?”

“No. Most were artists, our worldsmiths. I had to teach them how to paint by numbers like they were children, so we used a classroom in the castle. But they wanted to learn so it wasn’t a contest, though some are still struggling.” He sighed, acutely exhausted. Not Nora’s fault, for once.

She lunged at him. Or tried, sticking out her arm and barely budging. He swatted the tip of her blade with his blood-colored glove, brushing her aside, “You’ve set me up as your adversary. So hostile, but I promise we’re on the same side. I’ve read all your papers, always wanted to work with you.” Flattery. If he was going to push her buttons, she should push one of his. Glancing toward an icon, she flinched, pressed against the roof of her mouth and puffed like a quadriplegic.

Only then did she understand the truth: she was strapped in a chair back in reality, wired up to every digit. Band pulled over her eyes, plugs in her ears, mouthpiece to stop from swallowing her tongue.

Something clicked. She struck his shoulder, slashed at his stole, watched red cloth fall to the floor. Kept pressing that button, left hand going through the motions, attacking like autotune and landing uninspired hits. Tore a hole through his chest—a trivial triumph. Still felt good watching him crumple to the stone, staring as blood chased along the cracks. Looked up after hearing metal on metal; made her teeth hurt again. Opening the cell, Caleb stepped over his own corpse.

He came carrying a steel rod this time. Two meters, tall as he was. Knew how to use it too—hit Nora where her neck integrated into her torso. Instant migraine, felt like her head would roll into the next cell. As Caleb moved to strike her chest, she reached for the rod with her right hand, clicked the button with the purple spark.

CRACK.

The shock disarmed him—his steel hit stone still sparking. Seeing him stumble, her fist flew at his face. Should’ve landed a good punch, found herself fumbling for his collar instead. “If you’re not my enemy, why dress like one?” Nora asked as she got a grip and snapped at him.

Caleb grinned like a kid keeping secrets, smug creases visible beneath his beard. “Suffering is your guiding star, Nora. Did you expect me to take you to dinner?” he asked while reaching to retrieve, but not before Nora knocked his rod away with her heel. That stunt was rewarded with a chop to her core, his strike stinging her sternum. Stunned, her heart skipped. Searing spread to her extremities.

Nora bore through the pain. She tried pushing it out of bounds, beyond the stupid buttons. “Why does everything hurt so much? I thought drugs were supposed to be fun.”

“You know why, Nora. Probably read my paper on it.”

Maya winced, still seething. “If I did, it wasn’t memorable,” she said with intent to sting. Was this a duel or a debate? No reason it couldn’t be both. “But I get the point; pain is a high-priority, low-latency channel, hooked up to all the important places.”

“If you want to learn how to feel in this world, it has to hurt first,” Caleb said with practiced prose.

He was right; pain had a way of teaching immediate lessons. But scars could tell tales communicated across time. She swiped at his eye with the rusty rapier, slicing from brow to beard. There was no button for that, it had happened on her intention. She knew her boundaries now, where Mayalinn ended and the verse began—her edge was where the pain stopped.

Caleb, meanwhile, kept leaking from his face. Not just blood but tears. His hand glowed dark, touched the flesh and sealed it shut, trying to unlearn her lesson. By the time his eyes opened again, he was locked in his own cage, Mayalinn tearing down the hall.

Wet footsteps echoed until she reached the intersection, stopping to survey. The city was up the stairs in one direction, but leaving now was bad karma; one never abandons a dungeon before beating the boss.

Her attention turned down the remaining path. Three guards rushed in her direction, brandishing freshly forged sabers crafted from the Queen’s steel. She collected her pain into a ball of building heat. Fire had always felt unrefined to Maya, too imprecise in a world of wooden ships and unstable powder, but for now it made for a fine focus to release her rage. She sent a stream of flares fanning down the hall, setting the trio’s tabards alight.

The guards flailed as flames licked their tunics, but Nora wasn’t one to let advantage go unpressed. She stepped forward, plunging her rapier through the first chest. As he fell, she snatched the saber from his grasp and spun to parry strikes from the remaining two. So long starter weapon; hello stolen steel. Cold air crackled across Maya’s arm, her excitement electric. Unlike oxidized junk, this blade could conduct.

Steel sparked against steel as she battled both guards at once, channeling Maya’s magic along the blade. When one overextended, she severed his hand at the wrist. The weapon fell from his fingers, reclaimed by Mayalinn levitating it into her free hand. Now brandishing both, she made short work of the last, slashing his stomach while simultaneously slitting his throat.

She tapped the blades together, hers now. The icons flipped and shifted, representing new capabilities. So unnecessary; Nora needed no reminder what moves Maya was capable of, all flooding back from five years gone. Wishing these distractions away, she strode down the final hall, past empty cells and mold-covered corpses, surprised to see the Silent Queen’s prison in such a sorry state. The hall’s smell shocked her most; the metaverse was not supposed to stink. At the end awaited a door banded with black iron. Peeking in provoked unexpected dread, a sense of something hard to process. Those dissonant echoes of half-familiar madness crept in again, crawling along her neck.

Caleb came up behind her. “Don’t go unprepared. What’s in there wasn’t in the tutorial.”

Maya turned back. “I thought you wanted to hurt me. Why would you care?”

Coarse but sincere, Caleb replied, “I’d rather you didn’t die.”

“Will it kill me IRL?” Nora asked unsettled.

Caleb squinted. “No, that’s stupid. This is a California company, we’ve got OSHA to deal with,” Caleb said. “But it would be a pain to put Maya back together again. Guest users aren’t supposed to die in the development environment.”

She looked down at the scraps she wore, fit for a day one newb. Under-protected and in over her head. Nora knew better, yet let limbic longing command the cold dopamine of old addiction. Hooked in and freed from the unfamiliar, their systems now in sync, all of Maya’s old tricks on offer. Flexing fingers forced damp air to chill around her chest, creating a shell of fleeting frost. Her saber sparked an affixed torch to life, flickering yellow glow cast long shadows in the cells behind. Drawing them in with a twist of her wrist, Maya bent the darkness about her body until sheathed in nebulous shroud. Knowing these wards wouldn’t last, she dashed through the door.

Torchlight traced in behind her, ambient rays revealing a large stone chamber occluded only by Maya's diffuse silhouette. Shackled to an anchor was a shuffling soldier, covered by a blood-caked coat, stuck in the center of the room. Bound by the leg with weapon in hand, a gas mask meshed with warped lips while letting its lenses expose empty eyes. As Maya approached, it shifted in place, raising a hacked and butchered barrel. A blast of buckshot flew at the level of her face. Reflexively she slid sideways, stepping out of sync with her shadow before rematerializing five feet to her left. Her maneuver caught Caleb’s surprise as he stepped in behind her.

The soldier shifted again, firing toward her chest. Her frozen shell shattered as it absorbed most of the spray, but stray shot tore her shoulder through thin leather. Maya reached forward with her uninjured arm to shock the gun from the soldier’s hand, only to find her trick was pointless; weapon and man were one, stuck together at the stock. It lurched and swung its other arm. Thick, mistaken fingers extended and split into six slick tendrils shielded in sallow grey scales, the drooling pink of fresh flesh thirsting between. Their unexpected heft struck Maya at the waist and tossed her to the wall, her impact accompanied by the sound of crunching bone.

The soldier started aiming toward where she lay.

Switching tactics, a stream of icy daggers flew from between her fingers, freezing the mechanism before it could fire another shot. Caleb stepped forward and brought down his steel staff, smashing the brittle barrel and buying Maya time to float to her feet. The soldier swung towards her again, but both her blades were ready. Her lower guard deflected, the higher came down and cut deep. Pink bits fell to the floor. The remaining tendrils reeled back. A vulnerability lay exposed, opportunity for Maya to feint and lunge forward. Charging through the monster’s mask, a shock rang along her saber. The left lens cracked and snapped with a pop as smoke escaped the socket. The soldier’s gun-arm went limp while the tendrils twisted inward, regenerating the pink flesh by filling in with tiny triangles. She severed the tangle completely, only to watch the cleaved half crawl away in futile fits.

Caleb touched her shoulder where the shot had scoured it, starting the healing process. She withdrew her steel from the soldier’s skull and peeled away the mask, finding a scrawny thing that looked not entirely unlike herself, but with sickly skin and grossly greased curls bound beneath the strap. Once her shoulder stopped stinging, she shrugged off Caleb’s touch, claiming the creature’s ill-fitting overcoat by snapping it clean. It wasn’t Maya’s Victorian vintage duster, but it had a stark style of its own with big brass buttons. Another snap tailored her trophy taut. Satisfied, she slumped against the stone. “It’s wretched, and doesn’t quite fit with the theme,” she said, poking the creature’s face with her blade to gauge vitality and lividity. “Who made them?”

Caleb shook his head. “A kid claimed credit for the first hack, but now they’re cropping up all over and spreading by themselves. Jack wanted a fresh set of eyes.”

“When’d it start?” Nora asked, slipping into clinical assessment.

“After Blightfin Bay crashed on stream. Mostly in older zones like Feral Vale and Thunderbrush Thicket. Stormseye is still safe.”

So, Diego’s suspicions might be right about the timeline. “Jack can’t be happy,” she said, crouching by the body and inspecting what was left of its writhing limb.

“No, loved it. ‘Great content!’ he said, until they started showing up here. Infecting his billion-dollar baby.”

Maya circled the corpse, examining from every angle. “Hell of an introduction. You’re sure bad training data didn’t sneak in from the compromised clusters?” Training data. Of course. “Shit, that’s how you know so much about me,” she said, snapping back towards him.

Caleb nodded. “Everything Maya ever did, touched, or said, all stored in our datachains, back to the beginning. That feed was fed into StormGEN-03 during the initial training run. Your story is part of hers.”

Nora wanted to protest, but she was sure she’d signed her self away with a checkbox back when she was sixteen. “03 is a her?”

Caleb shrugged. “Not old enough to choose, I know. Doesn’t even have a persona yet, just a bundle of sensation crying out for more. But we’ve been making bets, and I know what my money is on.”

“So, you what? Read my SparkNotes? Got me all figured out?”

“Mayalinn Noramancer was, is, a complex character. Storms of Steel wouldn’t be the same without the time you spent here. By blending that with all available data on Nora Pierce, including your research, you weren’t hard to decipher.”

“Alright, tell me my score,” Maya said, shaking her head.

“You hate yourself. Push yourself just to punish yourself.”

Fuck. All that therapy when all she needed to do was ask an exascale AI and a weird guy with a beard. She shrugged, wishing it were so simple. Coughing green and croaking wet, the soldier’s head fell sideways. Maya’s blade tilted it back to face her, asking, “What are you?”

“The future,” it spit, and died.
